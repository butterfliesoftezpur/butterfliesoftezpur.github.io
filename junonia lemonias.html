  • Common name

    • Lemon Pansy

  • Field Characters

    • A brown butterfly with numerous eyespots as well as black and lemon-yellow spots and lines on the fore wings and hind wings. In the dry season the markings are reduced and in the wet season they are more prominent and larger. The colouration on the underside of the wings is also variable. In the wet season the markings are distinct and vivid, in the dry season they are obscure and pale, thus aiding the butterfly in camouflage among dried leaf-litter. The male and female look similar.

  • Status, Distribution and Habitat

    • TheLemon Pansy is among the commonest butterflies in gardens, fallow lands and open wooded areas. It is found almost throughout the year. Although it is more numerous during the monsoon and post-monsoon months, it maintains some population in cold and dry seasons as well. It is distributed in the Indian subcontinent, extending up to Malaysia in the east.

  • Habits

    • This is a very active butterfly. In themorning it is seen basking on low-growing herbs or on the ground. It rests with its wings spread flat and facing the sun. However, every now and then it changes its position or place. Afterwards, when it starts its activity, it selects a vantage point such as an outcropped branch and keeps an eye on the trespassing butterflies which it chases away. This habit is most pronounced in the Chocolate Pansy, but is also seen in all other species to various degrees. Once in a while it slowly moves its wings up and down, while thus perched.It is also an active feeder. It takes great delight in visiting flowers of Lantana, Cosmos or other large flowers with abundant nectar. While feeding, the wings are kept halfopen.The Lemon Pansy, like the other Pansies, is very easy toapproach, but it keeps a certain distance. If one goes too close, it darts ahead with rapid wingbeats, but settles soon and not very far away. It always flies close to the ground, wanders around for some time but may return to the same place. However, it is able to disperse efficiently and easily. Annual fires are a common phenomenon in Indian forests and grasslands. After the fire destroys the ground vegetation, this one of the first butterflies to appear in the burnt areas, usually within a week or so.

  • Wing expanse

    • 45-60 mm

  • Food plants

    • Being such a widely distributed species, it feeds on a variety of plants belonging to many families. Within Indian limits, Artemisia spp., Blumea spp. Debregeasia bicolor, Gnaphalium spp. and Tricholepis spp. are reported, besides Zornia gibbosa which is most common, but "probably many others" (Wynter-Blyth 1957).

  • Distribution

    • India: Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Punjab, Assam, Sikkim, West Bengal. Elsewhere: Myanmar, Sri Lanka.

  • Status

    • Common
