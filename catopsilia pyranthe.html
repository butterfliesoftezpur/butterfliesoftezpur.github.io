  • Common name

    • Mottled Emigrant

  • Field Characters

    • :

    The upper side is dull white and the underside is finely striated with light brown or dark grey. The male has a narrow black border at the apex of its wings as well as a small cell-end spot and its underside is tinged with faint green. The female has a broader black border and larger cell-end spot, its underside is tinged with yellow.

  • Status, Distribution and Habitat

    • :

    This species replaces the Common Emigrant in semi-arid and arid areas with little woody vegetation, but both may co-occur in dry, wooded habitats. It is abundant in the Deccan plateau on cultivated land, scrub, grasslands and savannahs. It also inhabits the dry foothills at the base of the Western Ghats and other hill-ranges. It is active throughout the year, but most abundant during the pre-monsoon and post-monsoon months. It is distributed in tropical Africa, south Asia, southeast Asia and parts of Australia.

  • Habits

    • :

    The Mottled Emigrant is an energetic butterfly which rarely rests during its activity period. It is most active during the morning and early afternoon, covering long distances in single flights. It flies close to the ground in a powerful and bounding manner similar to the Common Emigrant. It feeds on the flowers of herbs and shrubs, for example, Abutilon indicum, Boerhaavia diffusa, Caesalpinia coriaria, Cajanus cajan, Lantana camara, Medicago sativa, Momordica charantia, Punica granatum, Sesbania sesban, Sida cordifolia and Tagetes erecta. On hot days the males gather in great numbers on wet soil, forming dense mud- puddling assemblages. While resting, the wings are closed over the back but the fore wings are not drawn between the hind wings. This posture is maintained while basking also, but the butterfly usually leans on one side, so that the wing surface is perpendicular to the sun's rays. Because this species forms large hordes of freshly emerged individuals, it is a regularmigrant in the plains of peninsular India, travelling both long and short distances.

  • Larval Host Plants

    • :

    The caterpillars feed on plants of the family Fabaceae (= Papilionaceae). The recorded species are Cassia auriculata, C. fistula, C. tora, C.occidentalis and Sesbania bispinosa.

  • Wingspan:50-70 mm

  • Distribution

    • :

    This butterfly is commonly found in the lowlands, especially in the dry zone. It appears in hilly areas during migration. Medium-sized pieridbutterfly found in Asia, Cambodia and parts of Australia. The species gets its name from its habit of migration.
